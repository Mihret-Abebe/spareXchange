Abstract
SpareXChange is a cross-platform application designed to address the challenges of finding affordable and compatible spare parts for vehicles, machinery, and electronics. Many usable components are discarded, leading to waste and higher maintenance costs, while existing platforms fail to provide a community-driven, real-time exchange for spare parts. Our goal is to create a sustainable solution that connects users, garages, repair shops, recyclers, and electronics enthusiasts.
The platform will be accessible as both a web application and a mobile application, ensuring users can interact with it anytime and anywhere. Key functionalities will include real-time trade matching, an Eco Points system to encourage recycling and reuse, and a trust framework built around verified sellers and community engagement.
Users will benefit from an interactive and localized experience that promotes accessibility, sustainability, and a sense of community. By fostering a circular economy for spare parts, SpareXChange aims to reduce e-waste and improve resource allocation.
Through features that prioritize transparency, ease of use, and environmental responsibility, the platform will create a smarter ecosystem for individuals and businesses alike. Ultimately, SpareXChange bridges the gap between availability and affordability while addressing global waste challenges.



Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1. Introduction
In today’s world, finding affordable and compatible spare parts for vehicles, machinery, and electronics can be challenging. Many usable components are discarded, leading to unnecessary waste and higher maintenance costs. Existing platforms often fail to provide a real-time, community-driven exchange that connects buyers and sellers efficiently. This situation highlights the need for a system that simplifies access to spare parts while promoting sustainability and responsible resource use.
The proposed project, SpareXChange, aims to address these challenges by creating a cross-platform solution available as both a web application and a mobile application. The software will serve as a hub for users, garages, repair shops, recyclers, and electronics enthusiasts to buy, sell, and exchange spare parts in a secure and community-focused environment.
The primary objective of this project is to foster a circular economy for spare parts by enabling easy trade and reuse. Specific goals include implementing real-time trade matching, establishing a trust system for verified sellers, and introducing an Eco Points system to encourage recycling and sustainable practices.
The scope of the project includes the development of the platform’s user interface, database management system, and cross-platform functionality to ensure accessibility on multiple devices. The system will focus on usability, reliability, and environmental responsibility.
By creating a platform that bridges the gap between availability and affordability of spare parts, SpareXChange is expected to reduce e-waste, improve resource allocation, and provide an engaging experience for individuals and businesses alike. This project is motivated by the need for sustainable solutions in the maintenance and repair domain and the potential to enhance community-driven collaboration.
1.2. Background of the Project
The demand for spare parts for vehicles, machinery, and electronics has grown significantly due to increased usage and the need for regular maintenance. Despite this demand, many functional components are often discarded, resulting in unnecessary waste and higher costs for repairs. Individuals and businesses frequently struggle to find compatible parts in a timely and affordable manner, creating inefficiencies in the maintenance and repair process. 
Vehicles in Ethiopia: Context and Challenges
In Ethiopia, the automotive landscape is characterized by a high reliance on second-hand imported vehicles, which dominate the market. For a population of over 120 million, the vehicle population remains relatively low but has been growing at an average annual rate of approximately 9% over the last decade. Historically, iconic models like the Volkswagen Beetle (the "Love Bug") and the Toyota Corolla have defined the streets, with many of these vehicles remaining in service for decades.
However, maintaining these aging fleets presents significant challenges. The scarcity of authentic and affordable spare parts often leads to prolonged vehicle downtime. Furthermore, Ethiopia has recently taken a pioneering step by banning the import of internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles in favor of electric vehicles (EVs) to promote a green economy. This transition creates a dual challenge: the need to maintain existing ICE vehicles with a dwindling supply of parts and the emerging need for specialized components for the new EV fleet.
History and Limitations of Existing Systems
The existing spare parts ecosystem in Ethiopia has traditionally relied on fragmented, physical marketplaces such as the "Somali Tera" in Addis Ababa. While these hubs are central to the trade, they lack transparency, real-time inventory tracking, and formal trust mechanisms. Previous attempts to digitize this market through general classified platforms have improved visibility but failed to address the specific needs of the circular economy, such as reverse logistics and community-driven recycling. The absence of a unified platform that promotes accessibility, transparency, and sustainability has led to significant e-waste and resource misallocation.
By understanding the limitations of existing solutions and the growing need for a reliable and sustainable platform, the development of SpareXChange is both timely and necessary. The project builds on the lessons learned from prior systems while addressing their shortcomings to create a more effective and user-centred solution.
1.3. Statement of the Problem
Finding affordable and compatible spare parts for vehicles, machinery, and electronics remains a persistent challenge for individuals and businesses. Many usable components are discarded, resulting in unnecessary waste and increased maintenance costs. Users often face difficulties in locating parts quickly, verifying the reliability of sellers, and accessing affordable options, which creates inefficiencies in the maintenance and repair process.
Current systems and platforms provide limited solutions to this problem. They are often fragmented, lack real-time interaction, and fail to build a trustworthy, community-driven environment for exchanging spare parts. As a result, users may encounter delays, incomplete information, and limited opportunities to recycle or reuse components. These shortcomings highlight the need for a more organized and reliable platform.
The specific problem this project addresses is the lack of a unified, accessible, and interactive platform that connects users, garages, repair shops, and recyclers effectively. There is a clear gap in the market for a system that promotes transparency, facilitates real-time trade matching, and encourages sustainable practices through incentives like Eco Points.
This project addresses the following aspects:
1.The extent to which a cross-platform system can improve access to affordable and compatible spare parts for vehicles, machinery, and electronics.
2.The role of community-focused features in enhancing trust, reliability, and transparency in spare parts exchange.
3.    The effectiveness of a digital platform in promoting recycling and encouraging the sustainable reuse of spare parts.
By addressing these problems and questions, SpareXChange aims to provide a practical solution that bridges the gap between availability and affordability, reduces e-waste, and fosters a more sustainable and efficient ecosystem for spare parts.

Description of the existing system and Literature Review
2.1.  The Overview of the existing system
Ethiopia’s e-commerce scene is still early-stage but accelerating, with over 80+ e-commerce platforms operating, but most are small and earn less than $10k/year. These platforms mostly act as digital intermediaries that connect buyers and sellers of general goods, with some touching the spare-parts domain: Zemen Gebeya provides a national multi-vendor marketplace where businesses input product listings and customers place orders that are processed via integrated logistics and telecom services; Jiji Ethiopia functions as a classifieds platform where users upload item/parts ads and other users directly contact them, with no formal payment or delivery processing; Ubuy Ethiopia serves as a cross-border import channel where customers search for foreign products (including spare parts), place orders,he and t system handles international procurement, shipping, and delivery into Ethiopia, mainly for new branded items rather than local exchanges.
For spare parts, the pattern is: a lot of activity is still offline (dealers, Merkato, dedicated importers like Moenco, Pepo. But some marketplaces and even a dedicated “Ethio Spare Market” app are pushing auto parts online. Ethio Spare Market is only mobile-based online spare parts marketplace that primarily serves as a centralized catalog and ordering system for only automotive parts in Ethiopia, where users (mainly car owners and mechanics) input their vehicle details or browse categories like engine, electrical, suspension, and braking components, the app processes this by matching them with a curated inventory of genuine parts sourced from trusted suppliers, and outputs a straightforward purchase flow that lets customers select items and arrange orders, effectively digitizing the traditional spare parts shop experience but focusing on new, high-quality parts rather than peer-to-peer exchange, recycling, or community-driven reuse.
2.2. Users of the current system
Even though there are some platforms out there that focus on selling and buying spare parts, this section focuses on the platform called ‘Ethio Spare Market ’ since it is quite similar, in its focus on spare parts, to our platform. Ethio Spare Market primarily serves vehicle owners and individual consumers, along with mechanics and garages who need reliable automotive parts. Vehicle owners expect an easy way to search for compatible parts by car model or category, clear information on quality and compatibility, fair pricing, and dependable delivery or pickup. Mechanics and garages, on the other hand, need fast access to trusted parts so they can reduce repair time for customers, quickly check stock and prices while on the job, and ideally benefit from consistent availability and possibly bulk or loyalty advantages. For both groups, Ethio Spare Market’s core value is a streamlined, digital alternative to physically moving between multiple spare part shops.
On the supply side, spare parts suppliers, distributors, and importers use Ethio Spare Market as a digital sales channel to reach more customers, manage their inventory, and increase visibility in the market. Their expectations include tools to upload and maintain product listings, manage stock levels and pricing, and access basic insights into which items are in demand. The platform operators themselves need stable transaction volume, clean and accurate product data, satisfied users, and efficient onboarding of new sellers to keep the system sustainable and competitive. Logistics or delivery partners, when involved, expect clear, structured order information and predictable flows so they can fulfill deliveries reliably.
However, in the context of spare part exchange and recycling, several potential stakeholders are underserved or almost invisible in Ethio Spare Market’s current model. Recyclers, scrap yards, DIY electronics/auto enthusiasts, and environmentally conscious users have limited or no structured way to list, exchange, or acquire used, surplus, or refurbishable components. Their needs, such as trading used parts, sourcing cheap but functional components, or participating in a circular economy, are not actively supported through features like peer-to-peer exchanges, real-time matching, or recycling incentives. This is the gap that the SpareXChange system targets: transforming these currently passive or excluded stakeholders into active participants in a community-driven, reuse-focused ecosystem.
2.3. Drawbacks of the current system
The primary limitation of existing systems, in the context of spare part exchange and recycling, is that they function mainly as a one-directional sales channel for new parts rather than a true exchange ecosystem. The platforms digitise the traditional “go to a parts shop and buy something new” model, but they do not actively integrate the large volume of usable components sitting with vehicle owners, garages, and scrap dealers. Many parts that could be reused, resold, or repurposed never appear in the system at all. As a result, users are often pushed toward buying new items, which raises maintenance costs and indirectly contributes to higher levels of waste.
In terms of information quality and decision support, the platform also has limited accuracy, transparency, and feedback mechanisms. Compatibility between parts and specific vehicle models or systems is mostly handled through static listings and basic categorisation, which can be error-prone or incomplete. This can lead to incorrect orders, trial-and-error purchases, and a general lack of confidence among users who are not technically strong, reinforcing the perception that it’s “safer” to go offline and deal with known suppliers.
There are also notable gaps in trust, community features, and user protection, which reduce the system’s effectiveness and reliability. Seller verification, ratings, and dispute resolution are typically limited or underutilised, which means buyers have only partial visibility into a supplier’s track record and reliability. This inconsistency in user experience makes the system feel less predictable and less supportive when something goes wrong, undermining long-term trust and discouraging users from experimenting with more complex or critical purchases online.
Finally, some platforms' scope of stakeholders and incentives are misaligned with a circular economy vision. Important actors like recyclers, refurbishers, DIY enthusiasts, and environmentally conscious users are either weakly served or completely absent in the system’s design. There are no features for listing used or salvaged parts in a structured way, no reward system for recycling or reuse, and no real-time exchange or matching engine that connects those who have spare components with those who need them. In other words, the platform does not tackle the deeper systemic issues of reuse, resource optimisation, and community collaboration that a solution like SpareXChange aims to address.
2.4. Literature Review
The SpareXChange system rests in a crossroads of circular economies, spare part logistics, and marketplace design, focusing on the Ethiopian context. The current state of literature covers the phenomenal growth of electronics and automobiles in developing countries, the significance of a well-choreographed reverse logistics process in the context of spare parts, and the pivotal role of trust and compatibility in marketplace design in the digital environment. At the same time, it reveals a gap in the design of community-driven platforms and country-specific solutions in the context of Ethiopia and similar countries.
2.4.1 E-waste, vehicle parts, and the circular economy in Ethiopia
National reports and policy documents indicate that Ethiopia is experiencing a steady increase in electronic and automotive waste due to urbanisation, growing vehicle ownership, and imports of second‑hand electronics and vehicles [1], [2]. Much of this waste stream is handled through informal channels, with limited formal recycling infrastructure and weak data on volumes, recovery rates, and material flows [1]. As a result, many components that are still usable or refurbishable end up in scrap yards or landfills, while repair and maintenance costs remain high for end users [2]. Broader circular‑economy and e‑waste studies on African countries describe similar patterns: a strong informal culture of repair and reuse, but insufficient digital and institutional mechanisms to systematically capture and redistribute usable parts [3]. This context underlines the need for systems that extend product lifecycles and connect dispersed actors in the reuse and recycling chain, which directly motivates the SpareXChange concept.
2.4.2 Reverse logistics and circular spare-part flows
A substantial body of work in industrial engineering and operations management addresses reverse logistics and end‑of‑life product flows, particularly for automotive components. Studies of reverse logistics models for vehicles and spare parts argue that effective circular systems require well‑designed collection, sorting, and redistribution mechanisms involving manufacturers, dealers, repair shops, and recyclers [4], [5]. Research on end‑of‑life vehicle (ELV) supply chains highlights the importance of planning for component recovery, remanufacturing, and material recycling as part of a broader circular‑economy framework [6]. These works show that reverse logistics can be economically viable when there are clear incentives, information flows, and coordination mechanisms between stakeholders.
However, most of these studies assume relatively mature markets and institutional environments with strong regulatory frameworks and data availability. In contrast, literature on circular‑economy implementation in developing countries stresses barriers such as fragmented supply chains, limited formal collection systems, and low digitalisation of repair and scrap sectors [7]. For Ethiopia specifically, existing work recognises the potential value of capturing and reusing spare parts but offers few concrete digital, community‑level tools to operationalise such flows [1], [2]. This gap supports the need for platforms like SpareXChange that combine reverse‑logistics principles with local realities, lightweight data collection, and community incentives.
2.4.3 Compatibility and fitment: from static categories to structured data
A critical technical challenge in any spare‑part system is ensuring compatibility between parts and specific vehicles or devices. Industry practice and technical documentation from leading e‑commerce platforms and fitment data providers show that mature markets rely heavily on structured fitment models, including canonical vehicle lists (make, model, year, generation), engine codes, original equipment manufacturer (OEM) part numbers, and equivalence mappings between OEM and aftermarket parts [8], [9]. These sources indicate that enforcing structured fitment information at listing and search time significantly reduces wrong‑part purchases and returns [8], [9].
Academic work on product‑data quality similarly emphasises structured schemas, attribute constraints, and standardised identifiers as key to improving recommendation accuracy and reducing mismatches in online catalogues [10]. These approaches contrast sharply with simple category‑based listing, where sellers only choose broad categories such as “engine parts” or “filters,” leading to frequent errors and incomplete information. For emerging markets like Ethiopia, importing such fitment concepts—while adapting to incomplete data and older or imported vehicles—is crucial. The SpareXChange project explicitly adopts a structured fitment model and proposes to combine it with community feedback (“fits/doesn’t fit” confirmations) to improve compatibility knowledge over time progressively, addressing a core limitation of existing local platforms.
2.4.4 Marketplace design, trust, and reputation systems
A large body of research examines how online marketplaces build trust and manage risk through platform design. Studies on reputation systems, reviews, and ratings show that well‑designed feedback mechanisms can significantly influence buyer behaviour, mitigate information asymmetry, and support transactions for high‑risk or second‑hand goods [11]. Research on peer‑to‑peer markets (such as sharing‑economy platforms) highlights that layered trust signals—identity verification, badges, transaction histories, and third‑party attestations—are key to overcoming reluctance to transact with unknown individuals [12].
For second‑hand or refurbished items, empirical work demonstrates that quality uncertainty and lack of clear warranties are major barriers to adoption [13]. Platforms that provide structured dispute‑resolution procedures, clear return policies, and visible reputational consequences for bad actors achieve higher participation and satisfaction rates [14]. 
2.4.5 Spare-parts inventory management and demand uncertainty
Spare parts exhibit distinctive inventory characteristics: high stock‑keeping unit (SKU) variety, intermittent and low‑frequency demand, and potentially high criticality when a part is needed for urgent repair. The operations‑management literature proposes specialised models for spare‑parts forecasting and stock control, often combining historical demand data with classification techniques (critical vs. non‑critical, fast‑ vs. slow‑moving items) and cooperative strategies with suppliers [15], [16]. While much of this work targets formal inventory systems in manufacturing or maintenance organisations, several insights are relevant for a marketplace like SpareXChange.
First, capturing even basic transaction and search data (e.g., which parts are frequently requested but rarely supplied) can inform both supply‑side recruitment—encouraging certain sellers to stock or list specific items—and reverse‑logistics flows, by prioritising the collection of high‑demand used parts. Second, research suggests that decentralised and collaborative inventory management—where multiple agents share information—can reduce stockouts and excess inventory in spare‑part networks [16].
2.4.6 Identified gaps and implications for SpareXChange
Across these domains, several consistent gaps emerge that inform the design of SpareXChange. First, existing circular‑economy and reverse‑logistics frameworks are often developed for high‑income or data‑rich contexts and do not directly address the realities of largely informal, low-digitisation ecosystems such as Ethiopia’s repair and scrap sectors [7]. Second, while compatibility and fitment modelling are well understood in mature e‑commerce markets, there is limited applied research on combining structured fitment with community validation in environments where official data (e.g., vehicle‑identification-number databases) may be incomplete [8]–[10]. Third, the literature on trust and marketplace design provides strong evidence for reputation systems and feedback mechanisms, but there are few empirical studies focused specifically on peer‑to‑peer spare‑part exchange and recycling platforms in developing countries [11]–[14].
SpareXChange is positioned to address these gaps by integrating several strands of the literature into a coherent, context-aware design. It builds on reverse‑logistics and circular‑economy principles to create a digital hub for capturing and reusing spare parts; it leverages structured fitment models and feedback loops to improve compatibility over time; and it incorporates marketplace‑trust mechanisms and incentive structures (such as Eco Points) to encourage sustained participation by diverse stakeholders. 4.3. Design Goals
The SpareXChange system is designed following object-oriented software design principles with a modular software design that encourages maintainability, scalability, and user friendliness. The design goals for the system are as follows:
Modularity
The system is divided into distinct, self-contained modules: User Management, Listing Management, Search, Matching, Messaging, Eco Points, and Admin Panel. Every single one of these modules has a certain set of responsibilities that can be developed, tested, and maintained independently.
High Cohesion & Low Coupling
The components of the modules are cohesive, that is, they are interrelated to serve a particular purpose. The coupling is low, which means that when one module changes, there are minimal effects on other modules.
Abstraction
More complex subsystems, such as the matching engine and the notification service, are hidden behind clean and simple APIs. It is unnecessary for a user or other modules to be aware of how such systems are implemented.
Encapsulation
Data/behavior is packaged in a class (User, SpareListing, Notification). The use of getter/setter methods regulates access to the internal state of the class, thus ensuring the integrity/security of the data.
Inheritance & Polymorphism
 Inheritance is used to indicate user role hierarchy (User -> VerifiedUser -> Technician), which supports code reuse with a robust hierarchy. The polymorphism technique helps treat different user types in a uniform manner when necessary, such as notification sending or profile display. 
Reusability
 The components (for example, the authentication service component, the search filter UI component), utility methods, and API controllers are meant to be reused later on for web platforms, mobile platforms, and enhancing similar projects.
 Scalability
 The architecture is scalable horizontally to support a growth in traffic (for instance, via cloud infrastructure), while the database design and API architecture are scalable to support a growth in users, posts, and transactions.
 Security and Reliability
 Security is integrated into the design (including JWT authentication, encrypted storage of data, and input validation). The system is designed for high availability.
 Usability & Accessibility
 The UI/UX design is developed in such a way that it helps in easy navigation, keeps the number of steps per task short, and is responsive. The accessibility guidelines are followed in an effort to make it easily accessible to all.

4.5. Subsystem Decomposition
The SpareXChange system is decomposed into several logical subsystems to organize functionality, simplify development, and improve maintainability. Each subsystem groups related components, services, and data structures that collectively support a specific feature area of the platform. This decomposition reflects the system’s architectural layers and separates responsibilities clearly, enabling developers to work on different parts of the system independently while keeping the overall structure consistent.
The major subsystems in SpareXChange include the User Management Subsystem, Spare Listing Subsystem, Technician Subsystem, Recycling & Eco-Points Subsystem, Notification Subsystem, and Admin Management Subsystem. These subsystems interact through well-defined interfaces (primarily REST API endpoints), ensuring minimal coupling and consistent communication across the platform.
1. User Management Subsystem
The responsibilities include user registration and authentication, as well as managing different roles such as User, Verified User, Technician, and Recycler. This also encompasses profile updates and overseeing the verification workflow, which involves document uploads and verification reviews. The system includes an authentication controller, a user service, a verification handler, and a user database collection to support these functions seamlessly.
2. Spare Listing Subsystem
The responsibilities include creating spare listings for verified users only, as well as editing, viewing, and removing listings. Additionally, it involves searching and filtering listings and viewing listing owners. The process includes various components such as a listing controller, listing service, listing database collection, and a media storage handler, which could be either S3 or local storage.
3. Technician Subsystem
The responsibilities include managing the technician profile and availability, allowing users to request a technician, and efficiently receiving and responding to these requests. Additionally, it involves providing a comprehensive list of technicians and contains a technician controller, technician service, and the collection of a technician request database.
4. Recycling & Eco-Points Subsystem
The responsibilities include submitting recyclable items, validating recycler licenses, and updating eco-points, along with enabling map-based recycler discovery and redeeming points for verified users and technicians only. This includes the recycling controller, recycling service, and the collection of a recycling log database, as well as geolocation and map integration for enhanced functionality.
5. Notification Subsystem
The responsibilities associated with the notification system include managing system notifications such as technician request updates, verification statuses, and listing responses, as well as triggering alerts for important actions and sending real-time or queued messages. This system includes a notification controller, a notification service, optional background workers, and a notification database collection to ensure efficient communication and handling of notifications.
6. Admin Management Subsystem
The responsibilities include verifying users, technicians, and recyclers, moderating listings or user activity, handling license validation for recyclers, and viewing system logs and reports. The process includes various tools such as the admin controller, admin dashboard, admin service, and verification review tools to ensure efficient management and oversight.
7. System Infrastructure Subsystem
The responsibilities include managing database access, file storage, authentication tokens, logging and error handling, and application configuration. This includes the use of a MongoDB database, S3 storage for files, JWT authentication for secure access, and a centralized logging utility to monitor and manage application performance.

4.7 Persistent Data Management
4.7.1. Data Storage Technology
The proposed solution utilizes a NoSQL Document Database, specifically MongoDB, as its primary persistent store. This choice is based on the system's requirements for:
➔Flexibility and Agility: The NoSQL model supports rapid iteration and changes to the schema without downtime, which is essential for a growing marketplace platform.
➔Scalability: MongoDB's horizontal scaling capabilities are well-suited to managing the high volume of user data, listings, and transactions expected in a community-driven platform.
➔Complex Data Types: It efficiently handles complex, nested data types, such as the location objects (which include coordinates for geospatial indexing) and images arrays.
4.7.2. Data Retrieval and Querying
Efficient data access is ensured through strategic indexing and specialized query types:
➔Primary Key Lookups: All collections are indexed on the _id field (ObjectId) for rapid retrieval of specific documents.
➔Reference Indexing: Foreign key references (e.g., owner_id in listings, user_id in notifications) are indexed to support fast lookups (Population/Joins) across collections.
➔Search and Filtering: Indexes are created on frequently queried fields like email (unique index in users), title, category, and status.
➔Geospatial Queries: The location field in the users, listings, and technician_requests collections utilizes a 2d sphere index to enable efficient, proximity-based searches, allowing users to find parts or technicians within a specified radius (e.g., "listings within 5km").
4.7.3. Data Manipulation and Maintenance
Data is manipulated via standard CRUD (Create, Read, Update, Delete) operations, with specific policies for data integrity and system health:
➔Data Validation: All documents adhere to defined schema constraints (e.g., required fields, default values, and data type checks) enforced at the application layer to maintain data integrity.
➔Timestamping: The created_at and updated_at fields in every major collection are automatically managed to track the lifecycle of each document, aiding in auditing and synchronization.
➔Soft Deletion: Instead of permanently removing records, critical documents like listings and users use a soft deletion mechanism (e.g., changing status to deleted or adding a is_active: false flag), which preserves historical data for analytics and legal compliance.
➔Automated Maintenance: Scheduled database tasks will be implemented for:
◆Index Optimization: Regularly analyzing and optimizing indexes based on query patterns.
◆Data Archiving: Periodically moving old, non-critical data (e.g., completed transactions older than 5 years) to a lower-cost archive store to maintain performance of the main database.

4.10. Access Control
This section details the system's access control architecture, which is built on an industry-standard combination of secure authentication and a Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) model. This ensures that all users including individuals, repair shops, technicians, and recyclers can only access data and perform actions consistent with their assigned role and verification status.
4.10.1. Authentication and Identity Management
Authentication validates a user's identity before granting access to the system.
●Password Security: Passwords are not stored in plain text. They are secured using hashing mechanisms such as bcrypt before being persisted in the users collection's password_hash field.
●Token-Based Access: The system implements secure, stateless authentication using JSON Web Tokens (JWT). Upon successful login, the system creates a JWT token for secure communication.
●User Verification: Account verification is mandatory for elevated privileges. This is achieved through an email/phone, and ID and licence document confirmation process, where a code is sent to the user to verify the account after registration, and ID photo and license document uploading respectively. The verification status is tracked in the verification_status field of the users collection.
●Communication Security: All data transmission between the client (web/mobile app) and the server API is secured using SSL (Secure Sockets Layer).
4.10.2. Authorization and Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
The system enforces access policies based on the role field in the users collection. This field assigns specific privileges and capabilities to different user types.
Role	Database Field Value	Core Responsibilities & Permissions
Standard User	user	Can browse spare parts.
Verified User	verified_user	All Standard User permissions, plus the ability to post spare part listings. This role may also receive higher priority in search results.
Technician	technician	All Verified User permissions, plus the ability to receive and manage technician requests (tracked in technician_requests collection).
Recycler	recycler	Partner role with permission to verify recycling actions and create entries in the recycling_logs collection, which awards Eco Points.
Administrator	admin	Full system access for monitoring platform activity, resolving disputes, and managing all core collections.

The system incorporates defensive measures against common web vulnerabilities to protect user data and ensure transactional integrity.
